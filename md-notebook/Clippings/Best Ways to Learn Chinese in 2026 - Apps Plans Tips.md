Learning Chinese in 2026 is genuinely more achievable than ever — not because the language got easier, but because the tools got dramatically better. Here's what actually works.

## The Core Principles

Before apps and methods: the research consensus on what drives progress in Chinese.

1. **Comprehensible input** — you learn by consuming Chinese you almost understand, not by studying grammar rules
2. **Spaced repetition** — vocabulary sticks when reviewed at the right intervals, not drilled endlessly
3. **Characters require dedicated practice** — reading and writing Chinese characters doesn't happen automatically
4. **Tones are non-negotiable** — poor tones create miscommunication; address them from day one

## The Learning Stack

### Absolute Beginners (0–300 hours)

- **Characters**: Recognize-first approach — learn to read before writing
- **Vocabulary**: Anki or a dedicated SRS app with HSK 1–3 decks
- **Tones**: Pinyin drill app for the first two weeks
- **Grammar**: NPCR (New Practical Chinese Reader) or ChinesePod for structure
- **Daily target**: 30 minutes SRS + 30 minutes input

### Intermediate (300–1,500 hours)

- **Reading**: Graded readers, then native material with popup dictionaries
- **Listening**: Chinese dramas with Chinese subtitles (not English)
- **Speaking**: iTalki tutors for structured conversation practice
- **SRS**: Sentence mining from content you're consuming (Migaku, Anki + extension)

### Advanced (1,500+ hours)

At this level, immersion is the method. Living in Chinese — consuming news, podcasts, films — is more efficient than any structured course.

## The Character Question

Most learners underinvest in character literacy and hit a ceiling later. Recommendations:

- Learn stroke order for each character — not for aesthetics, but because it encodes visual memory
- Recognize traditional and simplified differences early (useful for travel and media)
- Aim for HSK 3 reading fluency (600 characters) before focusing heavily on listening/speaking

## The AI Tutor Factor

AI tutors (ChatGPT, Claude, language-specific apps) now provide near-instant feedback on grammar and sentence construction. They're excellent for:

- Quick grammar questions ("is this sentence natural?")
- Writing correction
- Vocabulary in context

They're not a substitute for speaking with humans — pronunciation feedback from AI is still unreliable.

## The Realistic Timeline

| Milestone | Study Hours | Notes |
|-----------|------------|-------|
| Basic survival Chinese | 150 hours | Greetings, numbers, directions |
| HSK 3 (B1 equivalent) | 600 hours | Basic conversations, reading |
| HSK 5 (B2-C1) | 2,000 hours | News comprehension, professional use |
| Near-native fluency | 3,000+ hours | With immersion |

Chinese takes roughly twice as long as French or Spanish for English speakers (FSI rating: Category IV). Plan accordingly.
