Spaced repetition is the closest thing to a cheat code in language learning. It's not magic — it's a scheduling algorithm that shows you vocabulary right before you're about to forget it, making review sessions maximally efficient.

## How Spaced Repetition Works

Traditional flashcard drilling repeats every card at the same interval regardless of how well you know it. Spaced repetition adjusts each card's review schedule based on how you performed:

- **Easy**: the card won't appear again for weeks or months
- **Hard**: the card reappears in hours or days
- **Failed**: the card resets to a short interval

The result: you spend almost no time reviewing words you know well, and most of your effort goes to words right at the edge of your memory. Studies consistently show SRS produces 2–4x better retention per study hour compared to fixed-interval drilling.

## The Science: The Forgetting Curve

Ebbinghaus's forgetting curve shows memory decays exponentially after learning. Each time you successfully recall something, the curve flattens — the word takes longer to fade next time. SRS automates this process across hundreds or thousands of vocabulary items simultaneously.

## Setting Up for Chinese

### Choose Your Software

- **Anki** — the gold standard, free, maximum control, cross-platform. Steep setup but unmatched flexibility.
- **Migaku** — built on Anki, adds sentence mining from native content and popup dictionaries.
- **Pleco** — built-in SRS for its dictionary cards. Great if you want SRS without Anki's complexity.
- **HelloChinese / Duolingo** — use some SRS principles but are less efficient for serious learners.

### Card Formats That Work

Three card types for Chinese vocabulary:

1. **Recognition cards**: Chinese character → meaning + pinyin (reading practice)
2. **Production cards**: English meaning → Chinese character (writing practice)
3. **Listening cards**: audio → meaning (listening practice)

For most learners, recognition-first is the right order. You need to be able to read before you can efficiently mine sentences for new cards.

### What to Put in Your Deck

Priority order:
1. **HSK vocabulary lists** — structured frequency list, good for beginners
2. **Sentence cards** — a full sentence in Chinese with one target word highlighted
3. **Words from content you're consuming** — highest retention because you've seen the word in context

Avoid: isolated character decks without meaning context, overly long cards with too much information.

## Daily Practice Protocol

- **Daily minimum**: 15–20 minutes of reviews, every day without exception
- **New cards**: 10–20 new cards per day (more leads to review debt)
- **Review debt**: if you skip days, reviews pile up fast — catching up feels punishing
- **Morning timing**: research suggests morning reviews after sleep have slightly better retention

The #1 mistake: adding too many new cards too fast. After two weeks, the daily review load becomes unsustainable and people quit.

## Common Mistakes

**Cramming new vocabulary without reviewing**: SRS only works if you show up for reviews consistently.

**Adding words without context**: Isolated words ("苹果 = apple") are much harder to retain than sentence cards that show usage.

**Using English as the cue**: For intermediate learners, Chinese-to-Chinese cards (definition in Chinese) build stronger associations.

**Ignoring tones in cards**: Always include tone marks (pinyin) and audio. Tone errors are the hardest habit to break later.

## How Long Until It Pays Off

Realistic expectations for HSK vocabulary acquisition with consistent SRS:

- HSK 1 (150 words): 1–2 months of daily practice
- HSK 3 (600 words): 4–6 months
- HSK 5 (2,500 words): 18–24 months

The compounding effect is real: adding 10 new words/day for a year is 3,650 words with dramatically less review time than any other method. The hard part is the first 60 days before momentum builds.
