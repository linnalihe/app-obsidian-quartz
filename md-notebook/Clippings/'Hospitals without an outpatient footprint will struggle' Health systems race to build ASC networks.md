"Demand in outpatient services is on the rise, and hospitals without an outpatient footprint will struggle." That quote, attributed to Kaufman Hall analysis of 2025 hospital performance, captures a structural shift that is reshaping health system strategy going into 2026.

## The Outpatient Shift

Surgical and clinical volume is moving out of the inpatient hospital setting and into ambulatory surgery centers, outpatient clinics, and hybrid facilities. The drivers:

- **Payer pressure**: Insurers actively direct patients to lower-cost outpatient settings and often require prior authorization for procedures done inpatient when an outpatient alternative exists
- **Patient preference**: Shorter stay, faster recovery, less disruption — patients prefer outpatient when given a choice
- **Technology enabling it**: Anesthesia advances, minimally invasive surgical techniques, and remote monitoring make it safe to send home patients who previously would have required overnight stays
- **Cost structure**: ASCs operate at 40–60% of hospital inpatient costs for the same procedures, which matters both for competitive pricing and for value-based contracts

## The ASC Race

Health systems that don't own outpatient surgical capacity risk losing volume to:
- Physician-owned ASCs (which compete directly for the same surgeons and patients)
- National ASC chains (AmSurg, Surgical Care Affiliates / UnitedHealth, USPI / Tenet)
- Private equity-backed specialty surgical centers

The strategic response: large health systems are building or acquiring ASC networks as rapidly as capital allows. The goal is to capture the outpatient migration rather than lose the volume to competitors.

## What This Means for Revenue Cycle

The outpatient shift has direct implications for hospital finance:

- **Lower revenue per episode**: Outpatient procedures reimburse at lower rates than inpatient equivalents, compressing per-procedure revenue even as volume grows
- **Different payer mix**: Outpatient settings attract commercially insured patients (healthier, elective surgery), which can actually improve margin mix
- **Billing complexity increases**: ASC billing codes and rules differ from hospital billing; RCM systems built for inpatient workflows need adaptation
- **Volume offset**: The question every CFO is asking — can outpatient volume growth offset inpatient margin compression?

## The Financial Reality

From Kaufman Hall's 2025 national hospital flash data: outpatient growth remained the defining trend even as inpatient margins compressed. Health systems with robust outpatient networks — and the billing infrastructure to capture that revenue efficiently — posted materially better financial performance than those still primarily inpatient-focused.

The implication for hospital strategy is clear: outpatient footprint is no longer optional. The question is whether to build, buy, or partner.
