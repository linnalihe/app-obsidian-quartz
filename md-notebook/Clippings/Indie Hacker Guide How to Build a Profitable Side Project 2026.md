The indie hacker game changed in 2025–2026. AI coding tools cut the time to ship from months to weeks. That means the bottleneck is no longer building — it's finding a problem worth solving and getting the first paying customers.

## Step 1: Find a Real Problem

The graveyard of indie projects is full of solutions looking for a problem. Profitable side projects almost always start with a pain the founder personally experienced.

Three validated sources for ideas:

1. **Your own workflow** — what do you do manually that could be automated?
2. **Communities** — Reddit threads, Discord servers, Slack groups where people complain about tools
3. **Existing products with bad reviews** — App Store reviews and G2/Capterra are goldmines for "I wish this tool could..."

The problem is real if someone is already paying for an inferior solution. Willingness to pay already exists — you just need to do it better or cheaper.

## Step 2: Validate Before Building

Talk to five potential customers before writing a line of code. You're looking for:

- Do they describe the problem without you prompting them?
- How are they solving it today?
- Would they pay for a better solution? How much?

A landing page with a waitlist and email capture is your cheapest MVP. If you can't get 50 signups from your personal network and one Reddit post, reconsider the idea.

## Step 3: Scope the MVP Ruthlessly

An MVP is not a bad version of your full product. It's the smallest thing that delivers one specific value proposition.

Framework:
1. List every feature you want to build
2. Circle the one feature that delivers the core value
3. Build only that
4. Ship it to your waitlist

Everything else is phase two.

## Step 4: Tech Stack for Speed

In 2026, the speed-optimized indie stack is:

- **Next.js** — fast to build, easy to host on Vercel
- **Supabase** — auth + database in one, generous free tier
- **Stripe** — payments without backend complexity
- **Resend** — transactional email that actually delivers

AI coding tools (Cursor, Claude Code) cut implementation time dramatically when you use a standard stack they understand well.

## Step 5: Launch Without Waiting for Perfect

Ship to your waitlist, not the world. The first version will be rough — that's fine. You need:

- Working core feature
- Payment flow (even if you invoice manually at first)
- A way to contact users when things break

Launch channels that work for indie projects:
- ProductHunt (pick Tuesday or Wednesday, have friends upvote at launch)
- Twitter/X (build in public, share progress updates)
- Relevant subreddits (r/SideProject, niche communities)
- Hacker News Show HN (once the product is solid)

## Step 6: Charge From Day One

The biggest mistake: offering a free tier before you know anyone values the product. Free users give you usage data but tell you nothing about whether people will pay.

Charge from the first user. Even a low price ($5–9/month) separates serious users from free-tier tire-kickers and gives you the feedback loop you actually need.

## The Economics of a Viable Side Project

| MRR | Subscribers needed | Assumptions |
|-----|-------------------|-------------|
| $500 | 100 @ $5/mo | Basic passive income |
| $2,000 | 200 @ $10/mo | Quit-able with low expenses |
| $5,000 | 167 @ $30/mo | Comfortable indie income |

The math is accessible. The hard part is months 1–6, before word of mouth kicks in.

## The AI Advantage in 2026

AI tools give indie hackers an unfair advantage over larger teams on speed. A solo developer with good AI tool use can:

- Ship features in hours that used to take days
- Handle customer support at scale with AI drafting
- Write copy, emails, and landing page text in minutes

The constraint has flipped: marketing and distribution are now harder than building.
